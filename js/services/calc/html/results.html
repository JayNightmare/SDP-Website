What your result means


  • 90+ mL/min/1.73m² (Normal or High Function)

    What it means: Your kidneys are functioning normally. No signs of chronic kidney disease (CKD).

    Next steps: Continue maintaining a healthy lifestyle. Regular check-ups are recommended if you’re at risk of kidney disease.

  • 60-89 mL/min/1.73m² (Mildly Decreased Function)

    What it means: Mildly reduced kidney function. This could be normal for older adults but may indicate early CKD.

    Next steps: Speak with your doctor if you have other risk factors for kidney disease (e.g., diabetes, high blood pressure).

  • 45-59 mL/min/1.73m² (Mild to Moderate Decrease)

    What it means: There is mild to moderate kidney damage. This is classified as stage 3A CKD.

    Next steps: Consult your healthcare provider for further tests, monitoring, and lifestyle changes to prevent further decline.

  • 30-44 mL/min/1.73m² (Moderate to Severe Decrease)

    What it means: Moderate to severe reduction in kidney function. This is classified as stage 3B CKD.

    Next steps: A healthcare professional may recommend additional tests, medications, or specialist care to manage the condition.

  • 15-29 mL/min/1.73m² (Severe Decrease)

    What it means: Severe loss of kidney function. This is classified as stage 4 CKD, indicating significant kidney damage.

    Next steps: Close monitoring and preparation for possible dialysis or transplant may be necessary. Contact a nephrologist immediately.

  • <15 mL/min/1.73m² (Kidney Failure)

    What it means: End-stage kidney disease (ESKD). Your kidneys are no longer able to function properly.

    Next steps: Immediate medical attention is required. Treatment options may include dialysis or kidney transplantation.
